# What a genius horse teaches us about statistics This is Clever Hans.

### What a genius horse teaches us about statistics 

This is Clever Hans.


Clever Hans with a German horse who learned how to answer difficult math questions. People would ask Clever Hans, "What's four squared?" And Hans would stamp his foot 16 times.

He could answer a range of mathematical questions. He also learned multiple languages, but math was like his forte.

Skeptics said "There's no way this horse knows math."

So Hans's owner agreed to have Hans tested by an expert including a the veterinarian and a circus trainer. The panel studied Clever Hans and came determined that the horse was, indeed, a genius. Clever Hans went on a tour of Europe and North America.

Strangely, Hans did not always answer questions consistently. He didn't do well if the person asking the question didn't know the correct answer.

For example, if someone asked Hans "What is square root of 16?" but they didn't know the answer, then Hans would stamp his foot and keep stamping beyond 4. Hans also did not do very well if the questioner was physically far away from Clever Hans.

This was odd because why should it matter to Hans if the questioner knew the answer or if the questioner was nearby? Maybe something else was going on.

Hans was clever but he did not know math. Instead, he learned to stamp his foot until the questioner stopped looking at his feet and looked up at Hans in amazement. Then, Hans would stop stamping as if to say "I guess I got the right answer." That's why the person asking the question had to know the answer because they had to show Hans he had reached the right answer.

Hans would react to the questioner's behavior. He didn't know math, but he did know how to identify faces. So, a surprised/shocked face signaled him to stop stomping and get a reward.

Hans's story shows how much humans want to believe there is a happened in things. We tend to impute causality into an explanation so that it makes more sense but often correlations are not what they first appear to be.

Data analysts can be very powerful and there can be a temptation to find relationships everywhere.

Anyone can input values in Excel and produce a regression. It's super easy to do. But the computer can't think "does that make sense?" It's easy to say these things go together and then they're statistically significant. So obviously this is meaningful, and then you go off and make some decision. but you could be fooled like the people who were fooled by Clever Hans.
